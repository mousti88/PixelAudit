# Classical Forensic Methods for AI-Image Detection

## 1. Gradient-Domain Analysis (Sobel + PCA)  
Real photographs carry camera-specific gradient patterns (from optics, sensor demosaicing, etc.) that AI generators do not reproduce.  For example, Liu *et al.* (2023) note “the HVS is very sensitive to gradients and textures, and a lot of information about the image is hidden in … gradients”【30†L144-L153】.  In practice, one converts an image to luminance, computes Sobel gradients (Gx, Gy), then builds statistics (e.g. magnitudes, orientations or co-occurrence histograms) over patches.  Applying PCA or another dimensionality reduction on these features (trained on real images) can reveal outliers: synthetic images often fall far from the “real” cluster.  **Implementable steps:**  
- Extract luminance (e.g. Y channel) and compute Sobel/Scharr gradients.  
- Compute gradient magnitude and orientation maps; summarize them (e.g. mean±std over patches, histograms, or the proposed GLDN descriptor)【30†L144-L153】.  
- Project feature-vectors (one per image or patch) into a PCA space fitted on real photos; measure distance to the real-image distribution (e.g. Mahalanobis distance or scoring).  
- **Evaluation:** Compare separation on datasets of real vs. GAN/diffusion images.  Report metrics like ROC-AUC (e.g. Zhu *et al.* achieved ~99% on uncompressed high-res images using Fourier features【53†L862-L870】 as a proxy for gradient anomalies).  
- **Limitations:** Gradient cues can be subtle and easily destroyed by blur or heavy JPEG compression.  In practice, Santopaolo’s experiments showed that even strong gradient differences are “easily destroyed by post-processing” like JPEG or resizing【32†L166-L174】.  Nevertheless, gradient/PCA analysis (sometimes with binning or histograms) provides an interpretable baseline that complements learning methods【30†L144-L153】【35†L114-L123】.  

## 2. Geometric Consistency (Vanishing Points)  
Real 3D scenes impose rigid perspective constraints: sets of parallel edges converge to one vanishing point, vanishing lines lie on a horizon, and consistent lighting/shadows emanate from coherent directions.  AI generators typically omit explicit geometry, often yielding inconsistent perspective.  Farid (2022) demonstrates that GAN/diffusion images frequently have *mismatched* vanishing points.  In his tests, “each set of parallel lines on the tiled floor are geometrically consistent, intersecting at a single vanishing point. The vanishing point of the parallel counter-top … is inconsistent with the vanishing line”【13†L291-L295】.  **Implementable steps:**  
- Detect linear edges (e.g. LSD or Hough on grayscale). Identify clusters of near-parallel lines on the image plane.  
- Compute candidate vanishing points from line intersections (2-line intersections or RANSAC over many lines).  
- Cluster vanishing points; measure spread/entropy of clusters or the extent to which points lie on common vanishing lines.  
- **Scoring:** A real photo typically has 1–3 dominant VPs with low dispersion; synthetic images often produce scattered “ghost” VPs. Quantify e.g. variance of VP cluster locations or how well points align on a line.  
- **Related work:** There is no large benchmark, but Farid’s analysis shows clear failures of perspectival consistency【13†L291-L295】.  A recent arXiv (“ControlVP”) even allows interactive correction of generated-image VPs, underlining the prevalence of VP errors.  
- **Limitations:** This requires strong straight-line structure in the scene; flat or natural scenes may not exhibit enough lines to compute VPs.  It also assumes a single dominant light or simple perspective; complex multi-plane scenes may confuse clustering.  

## 3. Reconstruction Error (Autoencoder/Manifold)  
**(Optional – uses a learned model.)** Train an autoencoder (or use a pretrained diffusion autoencoder) on real images only.  The idea is that real images lie near the training manifold, so an image from that distribution reconstructs well, whereas a generative image (off-manifold) yields higher pixel-wise error.  For example, the DIRE method uses a frozen text-to-image diffusion model to reconstruct input images and then uses the residual error for detection【45†L194-L202】.  In summary: “DIRE introduces reconstruction error as the discriminative feature… assuming that fake images can be reconstructed more accurately than real images”【45†L194-L202】.  **Implementable steps:**  
- Train or use a pretrained encoder–decoder (e.g. MAE, VAE, or diffusion) on a large corpus of real photographs.  
- For a test image, encode–decode it and compute a reconstruction error map (e.g. pixel-absolute error or SSIM).  
- Optionally aggregate this into features (mean error, histogram, error heatmap).  
- **Artifact outputs:** Save the reconstructed image and error heatmap. Fakes often produce spatially smooth reconstructions with large systematic residuals, whereas real images have sparser, more uniform errors【45†L194-L202】.  
- **Evaluation:** On benchmarks (e.g. GenImage, ForenSynths), compare average reconstruction error for real vs. fake.  DIRE and follow-ups report AUCs often >90% when test distribution matches training model, but error drops for unseen generators【45†L236-L244】.  
- **Limitations:** This is not strictly “classical” (it relies on learning). Its effectiveness depends on how well the autoencoder generalizes. In practice, GANs/diffusion can often be partially inverted by similar models, so careful design (e.g. caption-guided forensics【45†L194-L202】) or abstaining on unseen models might be needed.

## 4. Frequency-Domain Analysis (FFT/DCT)  
AI images often exhibit spectral anomalies (checkerboard or unnatural high-frequency content) that real images lack.  Forensics research exploits this: e.g. Zhang *et al.* (2019) used the Fourier spectrum of images (via DFT) to train a classifier on GAN artifacts【53†L842-L851】.  Dzanic *et al.* (2020) analyzed the **Fourier decay curve**: by converting an image’s DFT into polar coordinates and fitting a high-frequency decay parameter, they achieved 99.2% classification accuracy on uncompressed images【53†L862-L870】. Giudice *et al.* (2021) focused on JPEG-domain analysis: they applied a blockwise DCT and found that GANs leave “GAN-specific frequencies” in the AC coefficients. They fit Laplacian distributions to DCT coefficients and used the parameters to distinguish fakes【55†L27-L34】.  **Implementable steps:**  
- Compute the 2D FFT of the luminance channel. Visualize the log-magnitude (often symmetric). Compute a radial average (power vs. radius) or anisotropy metrics (difference among axes). GAN images may have spiky peaks or missing mid-frequencies.  
- Alternatively, compute a blockwise DCT (e.g. 8×8 as in JPEG). Extract histograms or fit distributions (e.g. a Laplacian) of AC coefficient magnitudes; many GANs produce heavier tails or specific frequency peaks【55†L27-L34】.  
- **Artifact outputs:** Save the log-spectrum image and radial-power plot. For GAN images, you may see excess low/high-frequency energy or unnatural periodic bands.  
- **Evaluation:** Compare frequency signatures of real vs. synthetic sets (e.g. Fourier spectra, DCT histograms). Zhang *et al.* and Dzanic *et al.* report near-100% accuracy in clean conditions【53†L862-L870】.  However, frequency features are sensitive to resizing and compression (Santopaolo 2024 notes many detectors degrade under JPEG).  
- **Limitations:** JPEG or downsampling can wash out high-frequency clues. Also, advanced models (e.g. StyleGAN3, or diffusion denoising) may mitigate some spectral artifacts. Still, combining FFT and DCT cues is a powerful “classical” approach supported by multiple studies【53†L842-L851】【55†L27-L34】.

## 5. Sensor Noise and CFA (PRNU) Consistency  
Real camera images inherit a unique sensor fingerprint (photo-response non-uniformity, PRNU) and typical demosaicing/color-filter-array (CFA) periodicity. Synthetic images do not originate from a physical sensor, so they usually lack these consistent patterns.  In classical forensics, PRNU is widely used for camera identification.  For generative-image detection, one can denoise the image and inspect the residual: real photos’ residuals show the stationary PRNU pattern, whereas generated images’ residuals are noise-like with no fixed pattern.  **Implementable steps:**  
- **Residual extraction:** Apply a high-pass or denoising filter (e.g. Wavelet denoising, BM3D) to extract the sensor-noise residual.  
- **PRNU test:** If a camera reference PRNU is available, correlate the residual with it to see if it matches a real sensor【60†L1-L4】. Lack of correlation suggests a fake.  
- **CFA periodicity:** Inspect the residual or green channel for a checkerboard demosaicing artifact; absence or irregularity indicates non-camera origin.  
- **Cross-channel noise:** Check if noise residuals in R, G, B correlate in the way real sensors produce (real sensors have certain color correlations due to demosaicing). Generated images often show uncorrelated noise.  
- **Evaluation:** Marra *et al.* (2019) extended PRNU ideas to GAN “fingerprints,” but more simply the absence of any PRNU is a red flag. No single metric is standard; one may use the peak-to-correlation energy (PCE) between residuals.  
- **Limitations:** Purely synthetic images by default lack PRNU, but if a fake image is uploaded and re-photographed, it could pick up a PRNU. Similarly, if an image is heavily filtered, PRNU cues can be obscured. Nonetheless, PRNU-based checks remain a robust camera-based filter【60†L1-L4】.

## 6. JPEG & Compression Forensics  
JPEG quantization leaves tell-tale statistics in real images, which synthetic or multi-compressed images may violate.  Classical forensics examines block boundaries, DCT histograms, and double-compression signatures. For AI images:  
- **Benford’s law:** Bonettini *et al.* (2020) showed that real images’ quantized DCT coefficients follow Benford’s law on the first digit, whereas GAN images deviate. They extract the leading-digit distribution of all DCT (JPEG) coefficients as features【65†L59-L64】.  
- **Block artifact analysis:** Run an edge detector on the de-quantized image to find JPEG block boundaries; synthetic images (or re-saved images) may show inconsistencies (e.g. misaligned blocks).  
- **DCT histogram features:** Compute histograms of DCT coefficients per block (or per channel). Real images often exhibit characteristic “peaks” at quantization steps; GAN images trained on many JPEG’d photos may blur these peaks【65†L59-L64】.  
- **Double compression:** In real pipelines, double-JPEG (save with Q1 then Q2) produces periodic DCT histogram patterns (Lin *et al.*, Bianchi *et al.*). Synthetic images (if directly stored once) will not show double-compression artifacts unless later edited. Checking for inconsistent double-compression patterns can signal editing or generative origins.  
- **Evaluation:** There is limited literature specifically on GAN vs. JPEG features, but the JPEG AI community highlights that AI-based compression introduces new artifacts【49†L58-L66】. Use metrics like consistency of quantization tables or entropy of DCT coefficients across the image to differentiate.  
- **Limitations:** Any JPEG forensic method assumes knowledge of original (real) quantization. Social-media re-compression will weaken these traces. JPEG AI (new codecs) further complicate assumptions. Still, Benford-first-digit on DCT (【65†L59-L64】) and block-grid ELA remain simple classical tests that add evidence.

## 7. Photometric/Optical Consistency Checks  
AI generators do not model physical optics or illumination physics, so forensics can check high-level plausibility:  
- **Illumination consistency:** Use photometric analysis (e.g. estimating dominant light direction via shading or spherical harmonics). Farid *et al.* (2022) found that diffusion images sometimes have plausible global lighting but “lighting within an image can be highly variable” in unnatural ways【71†L1-L4】.  Compute an illumination map (via shape-from-shading on faces, or shadow geometry) and check uniformity.  
- **Shadow geometry:** As Farid (2022) notes, cast shadows and vanishing points obey the same perspective rules【72†L1-L4】. Inconsistencies (e.g. multiple light sources with no reason) can be detected by extending shadow lines to check for a single light origin.  
- **Chromatic aberration / lens defects:** Real cameras often have slight color fringing at high-contrast edges. One could analyze edges across R/G/B channels for subpixel offsets (using correlation). GAN images usually lack realistic aberration; a null result can be a clue (though no standard threshold is established).  
- **Depth-of-field and focus:** Real photos may have defocus blur following lens optics (bokeh characteristics). Generated images often apply uniform sharpness. Detecting unrealistic sharpness distributions (e.g. no depth blur where expected) can signal a fake.  
- **Reflection consistency:** For mirrored or reflective surfaces, examine if the reflection obeys mirror geometry. Farid (2022) finds that reflections in GAN images are often “not fully consistent”【72†L108-L116】.  
- **Evaluation:** These checks are hard to quantify as single metrics. In practice, one would flag any severe violations (e.g. conflicting vanishing points【13†L291-L295】 or inversion of shadow directions) as strong forensic evidence. Tools like the Content Authenticity Initiative are developing automated lighting-consistency checks based on similar principles.  
- **Limitations:** Many real images themselves can violate ideal lighting (e.g. multi-illuminant scenes). These tests are best used in combination, not standalone. They provide intuitive visual cues (e.g. diagnostic maps of estimated light vector) rather than high-accuracy classifiers.

### Comparative Notes and Recommendations

Across studies, no single classical cue is foolproof, but combinations improve reliability. Gradient/PCA and frequency methods have shown >90% accuracy on clean data【53†L862-L870】【55†L27-L34】, but degrade under JPEG or resizing. PRNU and optical checks add orthogonal evidence but depend on image content. In practice, one should fuse multiple features. Key references for implementation details include Zhang *et al.* (2019) and Bonettini *et al.* (2020) for frequency methods【53†L842-L851】【65†L59-L64】, Liu *et al.* (2023) for gradient texture features【30†L144-L153】, and Farid (2022) for geometry/illumination analysis【13†L291-L295】【71†L1-L4】.  Open-source code is available for some (e.g. LSD line detector, implementations of PRNU extraction).  In summary, these classical forensic signals – gradients, geometry, spectrum, noise patterns, and photometric plausibility – each have theoretical backing in the literature, and a carefully engineered combination can strengthen AI-image detection.
